About the project

Who we are & who did what

We are six undergraduates in DIGHUM 100 (Professor Andressa Maia). The role statements below reflect the section each of us authored and led in the project.

Team & roles

MH
Michelle Hong
Hypotheses & thesis lead

Michelle is an Environmental Economics & Policy major with a minor in data science in the class of 2027.

Wrote the project’s working thesis and the predictions we set out to test. These cover how transfer and freshman experiences differ, in things like articulation gaps, compressed timelines, resource use, and financial need, and which of those claims our data can actually check.

ThesisHypotheses
JH
Jack Hu
Topic & data/methods lead

Jack is a Data Science major in the class of 2027.

Shaped the project's direction and led its analysis. He drafted the research question, set the theoretical framing, and ran the Python analysis that surfaced the core findings on on-time completion, major access, and GPA. He also built the four Tableau charts on the Analysis page.

Research questionsData sourcingMethods
Maria Ma
Historical context lead

Maria is a Data Science and Cognitive Science double major. Outside of class, she enjoys swimming, badminton, piano, and sailing.

Researched California’s Master Plan and the affordability case for community-college transfer, framing Berkeley admissions within the state’s promise of universal access. Also created the site’s illustrations: the Introduction’s branching journey map, the Master Plan trapezoid diagram, and the financial pressure chart by entry type.

Master PlanAffordabilityHistoryIllustrations
Lawrence Rhee
Synthesis & website lead

Lawrence is an EECS major in the class of 2026. In his free time he plays soccer and music.

Wrote the synthesizing argument and the conclusion, covering why the two pathways are not the same experience and what future research and campus support should address. He also built and assembled this public-facing website.

ArgumentConclusionWeb build
IW
Isabella Widosh
Context & representation lead

Isabella is a Data Science and Cognitive Science major. In her spare time, she likes to scrapbook, do aerials, and read.

Extended the data-feminist lens beyond Berkeley, gathering UC-system and national context (the National Student Clearinghouse tracking-transfer dashboard and the regional transfer map) to ask how transfer students are represented in college data.

Data feminismNational contextMaps
Jeddy Xu
Pipeline & sources lead

Jeddy is a Cognitive Science major in the class of 2027. He enjoys exploring coffee shops and nature.

Traced the statewide pipeline that produces transfer admits, working through California Community Colleges DataVista and DataMart and the California State Auditor’s report. He connected the data-feminism and visualization-ethics framing to what those systems choose to measure.

Ethical Considerations

The ethics of counting students

Comparing two pathways means choosing what to measure, what to put on screen, and who the comparison is really for. Every one of those choices has an ethical side, and we try to keep ours out in the open.

Counting is never neutral

Our two lenses are ethical commitments before they are analytical tools. Data feminism (Rezai) insists that categories like “transfer,” “freshman,” and “success” are socially constructed, “the product of cultural and historical sets of values and biases,” and that someone always decides whose experience gets to count. Visualization ethics (Hepworth and Church) asks us to weigh the “appropriateness, authenticity, veracity, and feasibility” of data before we display it, and to watch how a polished chart can make an institution’s categories look complete and inevitable. We try to honor both. We report each pathway on the clock Berkeley uses for it rather than forcing a single timeframe. We avoid comparisons the data cannot support. And we draw the GPA chart on a full axis so a small gap stays small.

The ethics of absence

The biggest ethical issue in this project is what the data leaves out. Berkeley’s public datasets measure outcomes, but not the conditions that produce them, like mentorship, research access, housing stability, financial aid, and a sense of belonging. Because those experiences go unrecorded, a transfer student’s harder road can look in the numbers like a personal shortfall rather than a structural one. Treating that silence as neutral would quietly blame students for conditions they did not choose. So we treat the absence as a finding. The dataset looks clean partly because the messier experiences never make it onto the page.

The state’s own auditors put a number on part of this absence. A 2024 audit found that only about 21 percent of California community college students who began college from 2017 to 2019 and intended to transfer did so within four years (California State Auditor, Report 2023-123). That is a narrower claim than “most transfer students fail to transfer,” and the narrowing matters. Of the roughly 745,000 students who did not transfer within four years, about 96 percent never applied to a CSU or UC campus at all, and 61 percent of those students had earned 30 or fewer units. The number that circulates in public conversation is the 21 percent. The number that would actually explain the gap, how many students got pushed out before they ever applied, sits three sentences deep in an audit most people will never open. That gap between the headline statistic and the mechanism behind it is exactly the kind of choice about what gets counted that Rezai asks us to notice.

The same audit looked directly at our campus. Berkeley and UCLA had the lowest transfer admission rates in the UC system during the audit period, 25 percent of all transfer applications, and Berkeley’s computer science major admitted transfer applicants at a 5 percent rate. In Fall 2022, the audit found, Berkeley denied 108 transfer applicants who had scored in the campus’s own second-highest recommendation tier, and Berkeley had not set a freshman-specific enrollment target for computer science until that same year, even as its transfer targets for the major stayed capped at 37 to 58 students annually (California State Auditor, Report 2023-123). This is not a hypothetical about gatekept majors. It is Berkeley’s own admissions data, examined by an outside auditor, showing a major our own chart already flags as low-transfer holding transfer students to a visibly different standard.

Housing is another example. A companion 2025 audit found that Berkeley houses just 24 percent of its enrolled students on campus, the lowest share of any UC campus, compared with 51 percent at UCLA (California State Auditor, Report 2024-111). The same audit cites research linking on-campus housing to higher GPAs and graduation rates, the two outcomes at the center of this project. Berkeley’s own graduation-rate and GPA dashboards do not report housing status at all, let alone by entry type. The information exists. It was just collected by an auditor instead of the campus, and it never made it into the dashboard we are working from.

How definitions decide who counts

Administrative definitions have ethical consequences. A “transfer to CSU or UC” metric counts only students who meet specific conditions, such as completing enough units, leaving the community-college system, and showing up in matched enrollment records. So it leaves out everyone who meant to transfer but never cleared those bars. When a metric like that gets presented as the transfer rate, the people it omits quietly drop out of the conversation, even though their experience is central to questions of access and equity.

Stakeholders and impact

Transfer & prospective students

Most directly affected. An incomplete picture can misinform a community-college student weighing the transfer route, or frame their later struggles as individual failure.

Berkeley administrators & faculty

Should use comparisons like ours to ask whether transfer students get enough support on a compressed timeline, not to rank or stigmatize a group.

Policymakers

The Master Plan’s promise of access gets judged by outcomes. Partial data can make a pathway look more equitable, or less, than it really is.

Us, the authors

We began with a hypothesis and must guard against reading the data to confirm it. Our framing choices are themselves interpretive acts.

Limitations

  • Aggregate only. We use published, aggregate figures, so we cannot see individual paths or variation within a group, and small subgroups may be hidden.
  • Not directly comparable. Time-to-degree differs by design, since transfers enter as juniors, so we compare against each pathway’s standard clock rather than head to head.
  • A snapshot, not a cause. The data shows patterns, not the reasons behind them, so we cannot pin a gap on any single cause.
  • Inherited categories. By comparing “freshman” and “transfer” at all, we repeat a binary that flattens the variety within each group.
  • Our standpoint. As six Berkeley undergraduates, our questions and emphases come from one particular vantage point.

Responsible next steps

Future work should fill these gaps with methods that respect the people behind the numbers. That could mean student interviews and surveys done with consent and care, and institutional data that reports conditions by pathway, not only outcomes. We are not trying to surveil transfer students. We are trying to make their circumstances visible enough that support can actually follow. Taking those circumstances seriously is what makes transfer access mean something in practice.

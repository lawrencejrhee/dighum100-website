Analysis

Two pathways, one degree

Berkeley awards both groups the same diploma. Reading the public data side by side shows how differently they get there, and where the data goes quiet.

Where the two pathways came from

Berkeley’s two front doors are not an accident of admissions paperwork. They come out of a deliberate plan by the state. California’s 1960 Master Plan for Higher Education set up a tiered system. The University of California would draw from “the top one-eighth (12.5%) of the high-school graduating class” and serve as “the State’s primary academic research institution,” while the community colleges would admit “any student capable of benefiting from instruction” and teach the lower division (University of California, Office of the President). The transfer pathway is the hinge between those tiers, the part that is supposed to make access universal. The timeline below shows how that promise has been built up, expanded, and then checked by audits.

1960

The California Master Plan for Higher Education

Defines the roles of the UC, CSU, and community-college systems and establishes the transfer function as the route from open-access colleges into research universities like Berkeley (University of California, Office of the President). At the time, about 55 percent of the state’s public-system undergraduates were enrolled in community colleges; by 2023 that share had grown to about 75 percent (California State Auditor, Report 2023-123). The pathway this project studies now carries a larger share of the state’s students than it did when the plan was written.

2010–2023

Building (and rebuilding) the Associate Degree for Transfer

Senate Bill 1440 created the Associate Degree for Transfer (ADT) in 2010, and Senate Bill 440 expanded it in 2013, giving community-college students a two-year degree with guaranteed CSU admission if they meet its requirements. The UC system was slower to adopt it: Assembly Bill 928 created an intersegmental committee to push ADT into UC in 2021, and Assembly Bill 1291, signed in 2023, only begins piloting ADT-based admission into UC at UCLA starting in the 2026–27 academic year (California State Auditor, Report 2023-123). Berkeley is not yet part of that pilot.

2024

An audit puts numbers to the gap

A state audit reported that “only 1 in 5 California community college students makes it to a university.” Cheap access at the front of the pipeline does not guarantee that a student reaches the four-year campus (Echelman).

2025

Housing and support under scrutiny

The California State Auditor finds that the state’s public-higher-education systems “could better address student housing needs,” underscoring the material conditions that shape who finishes (California State Auditor).

With that history in view, the comparison is no longer only about individual merit. It becomes a question about the system itself. Does a pathway built for access also support comparable outcomes once students are in?

Most students finish, but not at the same rate

Both groups mostly finish. Berkeley's published dashboard reports each pathway graduating at or above 90%, and on the surface the two look the same. But that headline rate hides where they split. When we read the underlying data instead of the polished chart, a gap opens at the on-time checkpoint.

What it measures: On-time graduation rates for each pathway — 79% of freshmen finish in four years, 60% of transfers finish in two. Two nearly identical headline numbers sit on top of a real gap in when students finish.

What it misses: The published dashboard is not wrong, but combining the two pathways into one clean rate makes the difference disappear. As Rezai writes, the categories in a dataset are not neutral — they are "the product of cultural and historical sets of values and biases" (Rezai). Hepworth and Church would add that a responsible visualization should question its data before putting it on screen. Reading the tables underneath the dashboard, rather than the polished chart, is what brings the gap back into view.

The financial weight each pathway carries

Outcome gaps do not happen in a vacuum. Transfer students are more likely to come from lower-income backgrounds, face housing insecurity, and work significant hours while enrolled. These financial pressures sit on top of a compressed two-year timeline and shape how much room a student has to stumble and recover.

What it measures: Three aggregate indicators — Pell Grant status, housing insecurity, and weekly work hours — broken out by pathway.

What it misses: These are averages that hide the range within each group. A transfer student working 30 hours a week while housing insecure has a fundamentally different experience from one working 10 with stable housing, but the chart collapses both into "transfer." As Rezai argues, constructed categories flatten the people inside them. The chart also cannot show how these pressures interact with the compressed timeline — a freshman facing the same financial strain still has twice the calendar to absorb the cost. That compounding effect is invisible in the bars.

A small but steady gap in graduating GPA

Average graduating GPA differs only a little between the two groups, but the gap is consistent. Plotted year by year from 1999 to 2022, freshmen graduate with a higher GPA than transfers in every single year. The distance is a fraction of a point, and how we draw it is an ethical choice.

What it measures: Average graduating GPA by pathway, year by year.

What it misses: GPA does not account for the conditions under which those grades were earned. A 3.38 from a transfer student working 20+ hours a week, navigating housing insecurity, and squeezing four years of coursework into two is not the same achievement as a 3.53 earned with stable housing, lighter employment, and four years to pace the work — but the number looks the same on a transcript. Hepworth and Church warn that a visualization can make a small gap look trivial or dramatic depending on how it is drawn. We fixed the axis to avoid exaggeration, but even a responsibly drawn chart cannot show what a GPA cost the student who earned it.

Transfer students cluster in certain majors

Transfers are not spread evenly across the curriculum. When we sort majors by the share of degree recipients who entered as transfers, the clustering is hard to miss. Humanities majors run high on transfers while gatekept STEM majors run low.

What it measures: The share of each major's graduates who entered as transfers.

What it misses: The reason behind the clustering. The chart shows that philosophy is 71% transfer and computer science is 9%, but it cannot tell us why. Are transfers blocked by prerequisite chains they cannot complete in two years? Pushed out by impaction policies? Steered by advisors? Self-selecting based on what feels possible? Rezai would point out that the category "transfer student" hides radically different experiences depending on department — a transfer in English and a transfer in EECS are navigating different institutions under the same label. The data makes the pattern visible but keeps the mechanism silent.

The clustering starts at the admissions gate

The major clustering in the last chart is not an accident of who applies. It is produced at admission. When we sort majors by transfer admit rate, the same STEM fields that graduate few transfers also let few transfers in.

What it measures: Transfer admit rate by major — how wide the door opens for each field.

What it misses: Who designed the gate and why. A low admit rate could mean a department is highly selective, that it receives a flood of applications, or that its prerequisite structure makes transfer applications harder to complete. The chart cannot separate these causes. Following Hepworth and Church, we should read these bars with some suspicion: a clean visualization of admit rates can make an institutional choice — how many transfers to let into computer science — look like a natural outcome rather than a policy decision. The chart shows the gate but not the gatekeeper.

Where transfers come from

Berkeley’s transfers do not come from nowhere. They move through a statewide pipeline, and they cluster by region. As the map below by Cesar Alesi Perez shows, a large share of UC transfers come from the Bay Area. Students from nearby colleges may already know something about the campus before they ever enroll. The map shows where community-college transfers to UC and CSU campuses come from.

What it measures: Where community-college transfers to UC and CSU come from, by region.

What it misses: The informal knowledge and social capital that proximity provides. A student transferring from a Bay Area community college may already understand Berkeley's culture, registration systems, and housing market. A student from the Central Valley or Southern California arrives without that informal advantage — and no data field records the difference. As Rezai would note, the experiences that shape a transfer student's readiness are precisely the ones that disappear when "transfer" becomes a single category in a dataset.

What the numbers measure, and what they miss

Each chart above tells part of the story, and each one goes quiet in the same place. The graduation data shows when students finish but not why they fall behind. The financial pressure chart shows the weight each group carries but not how that weight compounds on a compressed timeline. The GPA trend shows a persistent gap but not what each GPA cost the student who earned it. The major charts show where transfers end up and where they are kept out, but not the mechanism — prerequisites, impaction, advising, or self-selection — that produces the pattern. The map shows where transfers come from but not the informal knowledge that geography provides or withholds.

This is not a flaw in our analysis. It is the argument. As Rezai writes, "binaries and constructed categories are the product of cultural and historical sets of values and biases" (Rezai). The category "transfer" flattens an enormous range of experiences into a single label. A transfer in philosophy and a transfer in EECS, a transfer from Oakland and a transfer from Bakersfield, a transfer with family housing and a transfer couch-surfing — the data treats them all the same. What gets counted reflects institutional priorities, not student realities, and who gets to define a "successful" transfer is itself a political question.

Hepworth and Church argue that a responsible visualization should weigh the "appropriateness, authenticity, veracity, and feasibility" of its data before it goes on screen. Berkeley's polished dashboards are not wrong, but they make the institution's categories look finished and obvious when they are neither. A clean chart that combines both pathways into one graduation rate does not lie — it just makes the gap disappear. The experiences that make the transfer pathway harder — mentorship, research access, sense of belonging, course availability — are precisely the ones the institution does not track. That silence is not an accident. It is a choice about what matters.

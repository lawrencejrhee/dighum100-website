Methods

How we built the comparison

We wanted to compare the two pathways on their own terms instead of taking Berkeley’s default dashboard views at face value. That meant pulling the raw tables behind the charts, cleaning them in Python, and being honest about what the data can and cannot support.

On this page: Data sources · Working from raw tables · Cleaning & aggregation · Comparison metrics · Tools · What we did not measure

Data sources

Our core comparison comes from two UC Berkeley sources, supplemented by statewide and national context that helps situate Berkeley’s transfer population within a larger system.

Working from the raw tables instead of the finished charts

The Berkeley Data Digest presents its figures through polished Tableau dashboards. Rather than screenshotting those charts, we pulled the underlying tables behind several of them. This let us aggregate the data on our own terms, by entry type and by major, instead of accepting Berkeley’s default groupings. It also kept the data inspectable, so a reader can check our work.

Cleaning & aggregation

We used Python with pandas to clean and aggregate the CSV tables. The main cleaning and transformation steps were:

  • Loading each Data Digest / InfoCenter table and standardizing column names and entry-type labels (“freshman” vs. “transfer”) so the two sources could be compared consistently.
  • Filtering to the populations we actually compare (degree recipients, split by entry type), and dropping rows that mix pathways or that report suppressed or aggregate-only cells.
  • Aggregating to compute, per entry type: transfer share by major, graduation rate, and average graduating GPA.
  • Recording every decision (filters, groupings, exclusions) so the pipeline is reproducible.

Why we don’t compare time-to-degree directly

Transfers enter as juniors, so lining up their raw years-to-degree against freshmen would be misleading. Instead we compare each pathway against its own “standard” clock, which is about two years for transfers and four for freshmen, and we say so on every chart. We treat that decision as part of the method, not as fine print.

How the lenses drove our methods

These choices are about more than technique. Data feminism (Rezai) pushed us to keep the raw tables inspectable and to treat what the data leaves out as a finding rather than a gap. Visualization ethics (Hepworth and Church) shaped how we show results. We plot graduating GPA on a full 0 to 4.0 axis instead of a flattering truncated one, and we avoid a head-to-head time-to-degree comparison the data cannot honestly support.

The four measures

On-time graduation

Share of each entry cohort that graduates within its pathway's standard window: about four years for freshmen, two for transfers.

Graduating GPA

Average GPA at graduation by entry type, tracked over time.

Transfer admit rate by major

Share of transfer applicants admitted, broken out by major.

Transfer share by major

Transfer share of degree recipients within selected majors.

See all four rendered as interactive charts on the Analysis page.

Tools we used and why

Tool Used for Why this choice
Python + pandas Cleaning, filtering, and aggregating the raw CSV tables Reproducible, scriptable steps we can document and re-run, which is more transparent than editing spreadsheets by hand
Tableau Public Exploring the data and producing shareable, inspectable charts Keeps the underlying data viewable by the audience, matching our visualization-ethics commitment
HTML / CSS / JavaScript + Chart.js This public-facing website and its interactive charts Lightweight, open, and free to host on GitHub Pages. Charts read from a single editable data file

We used Python rather than R because the group was more comfortable with pandas for tabular cleaning, and that shows up most in the aggregation steps above. We also kept the raw tables instead of relying on pre-rendered dashboard images. The Data Critique says more about why.

What we deliberately did not measure

The published data measures outcomes, but it says little about the conditions that produce them. Things like incoming preparation, faculty contact, housing stability, and financial aid are not reported in a way that compares the two pathways. We treat that silence as a finding rather than something to paper over. The Data Critique and Ethical Considerations take it up in more detail.

Comparing Freshman and Transfer Outcomes at UC Berkeley

Most people think of Berkeley admission as one door that you either get through or you don’t, but there are really two of them. This project follows freshman and transfer admits after they walk through, and asks how their paths compare.

Read the analysis How we did it

Our research question

Berkeley admits students two ways. A freshman applicant is usually a graduating high-school senior. A transfer applicant is usually a California community-college student who finished lower-division coursework before applying. They apply under different requirements, come from separate pools, and spend different amounts of time on campus, yet they leave with the same Berkeley degree. We wanted to set the two records next to each other and measure the gap instead of assuming it.

Question 1

The outcomes. How do graduation rates and time-to-degree differ across the two admission pathways at Berkeley?

Question 2

The data itself. What do UC’s public datasets show, and what do they hide, about the experiences behind those outcomes?

Why it matters

The University of California runs one of the largest transfer systems in the country, and Berkeley’s two pathways grew out of the state’s long history of public higher education. The transfer route was built as a kind of promise. A student who does not get in as a freshman should still have a real shot at a top research university. But getting through the door is not the same as having the same experience once you are inside. A transfer student often has just two years to sort out major prerequisites, housing, financial aid, and registration while also learning how the campus works. A freshman has four years for the same tasks, with more room to switch majors or find research. Those differences in the pathway end up shaping the outcomes that show up later in the data.

We argue that the two pathways are not the same experience wearing different labels. Gaps in graduation rate, time-to-degree, GPA, and major completion come partly from how individual students do, but they also come from the conditions each pathway builds in before a student ever arrives.

Theoretical framing

We do not treat the numbers as neutral. Two lenses guide how we read them.

Data feminism

Following Yasamin Rezai, we ask who gets to define a “successful” transfer and whose experience gets harder to see once transfer becomes a single number. The categories in a dataset are not neutral. As Rezai writes, “binaries and constructed categories are the product of cultural and historical sets of values and biases” (Rezai).

Visualization ethics

Following Hepworth and Church, we read polished charts with some suspicion. They argue that a responsible visualization should weigh the “appropriateness, authenticity, veracity, and feasibility” of its data before it goes on screen. A clean chart can make an institution’s categories look finished and obvious when they are neither.

Seen through these two lenses, the gaps in Berkeley’s public data start to look less like accidents and more like decisions about what is worth counting. We treat those gaps as part of our evidence, not as missing numbers to apologize for.

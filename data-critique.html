Data Critique

What the data shows, and what it leaves out

Every dataset makes a quiet argument about what is worth counting. Before we let Berkeley’s numbers speak, we want to know where they came from, what they can show, and what they leave out.

Where the data comes from

This project uses existing, open-access institutional data rather than data we gathered ourselves. The core tables come from UC Berkeley’s Office of Planning and Analysis, published through the Berkeley Data Digest, and from the University of California’s system-wide UC InfoCenter. Both report admit counts, enrollment counts, graduation and retention rates, time-to-degree, and degree recipients by major, each broken out by entry type. We found the figures by working through the Data Digest dashboards and exporting the tables behind them, then added statewide pipeline data from the California Community Colleges Chancellor’s Office (DataVista and DataMart), the California State Auditor, and national outcomes from the National Student Clearinghouse. We did not contact any human subjects. Everything here is aggregate and already public.

What the data can show us

Because these sources report outcomes separately for freshman and transfer admits, we can compare the two pathways with evidence instead of guessing. We can see how graduation rates differ, how long each group takes to finish against its own “standard” clock, how graduating GPA lines up, and where transfer students cluster by major. Set next to the Master Plan’s promise of access and the affordability case for community college, the data can tell us whether the transfer route delivers more than entry, and whether the outcomes are really comparable. That question matters to anyone choosing a way into Berkeley.

What it can’t show, and why that matters

The published data measures outcomes, but it says almost nothing about the conditions that produce them. Things like incoming preparation, faculty mentorship, housing stability, financial aid, and a sense of belonging are either unpublished or not reported in a way that compares the two pathways. That makes the dataset look clean and complete, largely because the messier parts of student life never appear in it. Following data feminism, we read this absence as a choice about what gets counted rather than a neutral gap (Rezai). The categories themselves, like “transfer,” “on-time,” and “success,” are built by people, and they decide whose experience becomes visible. The definitions used by the Clearinghouse and DataVista show how this works. A “transfer to CSU or UC” counts only students who meet specific administrative conditions, not everyone who set out to transfer. Someone who wanted to transfer but never cleared those conditions simply does not appear.

Our own assumptions and biases

We are six Berkeley undergraduates, and our standpoint shapes this project. We started with a hunch, that transfer students face a harder and more compressed road, and there is always a risk of reading the data to confirm what you already believe. We have tried to guard against that in a few ways. We state our thesis openly. We refuse comparisons the data cannot support, like raw time-to-degree across pathways. And we draw the charts honestly. Our GPA figure, for example, uses a full 0 to 4.0 axis rather than a zoomed-in one that would make a small gap look dramatic, which follows Hepworth and Church’s visualization ethics. Our choice of majors, sources, and framing is interpretive too, and a different group could put different numbers in front. We also inherit the institutions’ categories. By comparing “freshman” and “transfer” at all, we repeat a binary that flattens the variety inside each group.

Bottom line

Berkeley’s data is trustworthy for what it measures and genuinely useful for comparing outcomes across the two pathways. It is also partial by design. We offer it as a starting point for questions about support and equity, not as the final word on the transfer experience, and we count its silences as findings of their own.
